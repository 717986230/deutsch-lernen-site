# Feldstudie（田野研究）

**Philosophy: the tenderness of systematic observation.**

Feldstudie treats an invisible subject — a sound, a habit, a slow accumulation of
knowledge — as though it were a botanical specimen worthy of a museum plate. The
canvas is a laboratory sheet from an imaginary discipline: registration marks at
the corners, a numbered plate, a single specimen enlarged to monumental scale and
studied with reverence. Meaning is carried by form and repetition, never by
paragraphs. One glyph may fill half the page; one row of small marks may carry an
entire narrative of progress. Everything else is air.

Space is the first material. Wide, deliberate margins frame the sheet the way a
mat frames a print; elements sit on an invisible column grid and never touch. The
composition breathes in three registers — a whispered clinical header, a
monumental specimen, a patient field of repeated marks — separated by calm bands
of emptiness. Nothing overlaps, nothing crowds; every interval is chosen, measured,
and re-measured, the way a master printer checks a plate a hundred times before
committing ink.

Color is one living hue on warm paper. A vivid botanical green — the green of new
growth — does all the speaking, supported only by a deep ink tone for text and a
near-invisible grid gray. The palette is disciplined to the point of austerity, so
that when the green appears it feels like life itself arriving on the page. A
single narrow accent of foreign color may appear once, small and quiet, like a
pressed ribbon marking a page — never more.

Rhythm comes from accumulation. Small identical marks — dots, cells, steps —
repeat in ordered rows like drill exercises in a practice book, each one drawn
with the same care as the last. Their gradual filling, growing, or ascending *is*
the message: patience made visible. The repetition must feel hand-tended, the
product of painstaking attention, as though someone at the absolute top of their
craft placed every mark and stepped back to check its alignment before placing
the next.

Typography is measurement, not decoration. Thin geometric capitals, generously
letterspaced, label the sheet like instrument markings; a mono face records
numbers and coordinates; text appears only where a label is essential, small and
exact. The one permitted large typographic gesture is the specimen itself — a
letterform studied as an object, its curves rendered with the fidelity of a
scientific illustration. All else stays quiet.

The finished sheet must read as an artifact: meticulously crafted, labored over,
unmistakably the work of deep expertise — proof that something as ephemeral as
learning can be pinned, measured, and admired. If any element looks accidental,
it is wrong. Refine until the whole page feels inevitable.
